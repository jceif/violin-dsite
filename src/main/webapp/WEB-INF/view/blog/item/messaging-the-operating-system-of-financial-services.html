Messaging, the operating system of financial services

Koray Can Oztekin

I’ve never liked having more than two windows open on my desktop at one time, it’s too much. Call me a typical technology worker - headphones on to ensure sanity, multiple desktop instances to carefully isolate tasks. But as a product guy, I know that my style is not exactly typical for financial professionals.

Traders, for instance, typically have six screens flashing blinding colors all at once, coupled with frantic phone calls. Research analysts go deep with peer analysis and spreadsheets for valuation while simultaneously keeping their eye on macro data or real-time news around earnings announcements or new product launches. As I learned first-hand during my short stint in capital markets early in my career, this frenzy is not for me.

But that dependence on multiple screens is something we’ve studied. Why is it that traders and analysts are so dependent on those screens? It’s simple. This is where they connect to the entire industry to message, read and share content day in, day out: answer questions from their customers and colleagues, read market-moving news and reports, analyze company data and share them, adding their own opinions in more than 100 conversations. They make investment decisions and even integrate with their trading systems to execute transactions. Messaging is the operating system of financial services.

This is not yet the case for other industries, although the use of messaging is clearly growing for the broader market - the ratio is still about 50 messages/day, compared to 500 messages/day for financial services.

So why did messaging become so important for financial services, and what are the key benefits it delivers to the industry?

Quick draw artists work with speed and precision

All information workers want to get things done fast and ideally without mistakes. For those in the financial markets, this is even more of a concern given the value of the transactions and strict regulations. The sell-side competes to give the best advice and price within the shortest amount of time - otherwise it costs them the trade or, even worse, future trades. That’s why salespeople are constantly monitoring news from hundreds of sources and countless “trade idea” chat rooms to see if anything interesting pops. Then, with just a few keystrokes, they can broadcast updates to their entire clientele hoping to start a transaction. Some brokers even send messages every 15 to 30 seconds: imagine the level of insanity.

This level of traffic and frequency drove the need for more programmatic interaction. Years ago, way before team messaging apps, financial services introduced the idea of messaging BOTs. These BOTs programmatically capture market moving events and instantly broadcast information to both internal chat rooms to drive intelligence sharing, and to external chat rooms to generate trade ideas. They have extremely sophisticated filtering models so that they can create custom alerts for high-priority content and easily differentiate signals from noise.

Content drives the context

Whether you are on the sell-side or buy-side, your workday is a series of questions around anticipating a market direction, picking a company, the index, the portfolio etc. We know that users constantly read to find relevant content and discover value. The delivery speed of the content or the content itself can make a massive difference — remember how Selerity delivered Twitter’s earnings results (ironically on Twitter) faster than anyone else and caused that sudden sell -off? Whether it’s a piece of news or an update from the internal system, being able to quickly scan that content and easily share it in a message is critical and drives value.

It’s about your community

As a product manager, I need to interact constantly with my marketers, designers, developers - as well as my customers. This is no different for financial markets professionals, except for the size of the community that drives value to the business. Think really big! Broker - dealers need to regularly talk business with their customers, asset managers and traders to share trade ideas and pricing data. How does this happen now? Through those six screens.

This is what every messaging platform must strive for: to be at the center of the business process, regardless of the industry they’re focussing on. And, as you probably guessed, this is what we are building at Symphony - the single inbox where you can run all your business operations.
