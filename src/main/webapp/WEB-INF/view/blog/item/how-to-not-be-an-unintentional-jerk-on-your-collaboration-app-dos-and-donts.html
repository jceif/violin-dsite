How to not be an (unintentional) jerk on your collaboration app: Do's and Dont's

Koray Can Oztekin

Let's evolve the social contract.

It used to be so simple. You get an email, you decide the urgency and respond when appropriate. They’d call if it was that important. We all knew the rules around what’s “real-time” and what is not. But this has completely changed with the vast adoption of group chat.

IM (instant message) and group chat, traditionally a mid-way complementary tool to email and calling, are used increasingly by many as their primary communication methods at work. With the broader adoption of mobile in business and group collaboration tools, all messages are ending up in chat, blurring the lines between what requires an urgent reaction and what doesn’t.

Before: An instant message implied a sense of time sensitivity that was less than a call but certainly higher than e-mail. Now an IM means....who knows?

This new world order brings a new set of rules. We just don’t know them yet.

So, for all of our sakes (and for the sanity of your customers and work buddies) - behold, the do’s and don’ts of group collaboration:

Don’t @mention people just because you can - otherwise people will start muting your @mentions (only @mention when it’s truly urgent or a very busy chat room so we can all become accustomed with ascribing urgency to IMs in this way)

Do take advantage of the persistent nature of IM and group chats. Resist creating a separate conversation for every single message exchange and topic to avoid turning the system into a junkyard of abandoned conversations

Don’t send a message for every single entry — there is no reason for a single message with three lines to create a badge with four unread count. In the end, it is a single message, write it all up and then hit enter.

14:36 Me: Hey
14:36 Me: I was thinking of running to philz and catch up there
14:36 Me: What do you think
14:36 Me: ?

Do post to your Wall when you make FYI announcements so that they are only delivered to those who are actually following you (and interested in what you have to say)

Don’t post in a chat room and instantly forward the same message into a 1:1 to get immediate attention (annoying)!

Do send a blast when you really have something unique to send (certainly not the same research report that your analyst sent earlier)

Don’t chime people unless you want it to be the equivalent of yelling in their ears. In the case of latter, please do! :) Take a look at the user's presence before @mentioning/chiming them. For instance, if they are being shown as busy, respect that.

Do customize your filters and alerts to surface topics you really care about or need to respond quickly about

Changing our habits isn’t easy, and building such a social contract takes time. But tools are only as efficient as how they are used. So use these tips and I promise, you and your colleagues will thank me later.
