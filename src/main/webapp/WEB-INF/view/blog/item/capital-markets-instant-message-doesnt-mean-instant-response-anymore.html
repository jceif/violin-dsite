Capital markets: Instant message doesn’t mean instant response (anymore)

Koray Can Oztekin

When working in capital markets, being glued to the desk is part of the job. The competition is fierce, so the constant fear of missing a trade if every.single.message isn’t read or you weren’t at your desk when you needed to be persists. Every single “…” (typing indicator) counts - and instant message and chatroom content is fed to us in parallel, sort of like being on multiple conference calls at the same time.

These messages carry the assumption of instant response. When you consider how much information is thrown at you, this becomes a real challenge.

In the US Equity markets, asset managers typically work with an average of 40 to 60 brokers across research, advisory and trading services. Combined with even more fintech companies selling news or data analytics, this means constant communication abuse over email, chat and phone from almost a hundred sources.

Take, for instance, the morning routine of an asset manager who starts every day with a packed inbox:

  • More than 100 new emails - only three of which are worth reading fully and sharing with others
  • Morning notes from internal departments and brokers
  • Several research reports that are first emailed by the research team of the broker, then also blasted by the salesperson of the broker in snippets (one might call this spam)

This level of repetition, noise and urgency certainly kills the productivity on both sides of the market. The good news is that this is changing (albeit slowly) and automation is taking over the tasks that require repetition and instant reaction.

Pin-drop quiet trading floors

Electronic trading is increasingly the norm across all asset classes. This means that instant messaging and phone calling are used more to communicate market color and analysis rather than actual trade execution.

My projection is that in the next ten years, the role of the capital markets professional will be completely transformed. Rather than managing assets herself, an asset manager will explain how a Robo-advisor manages the portfolio, and a salesperson will focus on selling the trading platform and research system instead of blasting customers with prices and research reports.

Brokers will center in on building and fostering relationships to create value, leaving repetitive tasks like price distribution and trade execution to electronic systems. As a result, pin-drop quiet trading floors will become the norm.

As this not only requires technology improvements but also a culture shift, the change will not happen overnight. It will take time for financial professionals to accept that instant message doesn’t mean instant response. Blast overuse, a flood of chat rooms and single word messages will still create noise and clutter.

We will continue to build features to help the industry deal with this noise. Here is how you can use Symphony today to gradually begin to shift away from instant response:

Turn off your notifications to avoid constant context switch

Simply turn off all your notifications. Enjoy the zen and then carefully turn on notifications only for those conversations that truly matter.

Organize your conversations to keep most important ones at a glance

  • Create folders: This is an old trick from email - simply group all chat conversations under folders based on their priority
  • Customize aliases & notification colors: I assign green to my most important customers and blue to my favorite colleagues. But that’s just me :)
  • Customize alerts: Only turn on blinking alerts and pop out notifications for the chat rooms that require immediate action

Turn on Signals

Signals allow users to structure their communications and shift their attention to a conversation only when it’s absolutely needed (i.e. a Signal is received.) It truly unlocks the content of the conversation so that you can pull the information that you need vs. the overwhelming push of traditional IM or a chat room where a notification is received for every single message. Signals filter a particular set of #’s and $’s and only those messages containing the # or $ will create a notification, i.e., the signal. You can even turn off all alerts and just turn on the Signals.

Use these tips and reach out to me on Symphony to let me know what you think.

As the industry shifts away from previous communications methods, it’s also important that we build a new social contract around messaging. That’s for our next post.
