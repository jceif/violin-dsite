One in Three Healthcare Customers Will Have Records Breached in 2016 – Are You Ready?

Karen Brooks

On a recent visit to a new doctor, I arrived the requisite 15 minutes early, expecting to be greeted with the usual clipboard of grainy photocopied forms to fill out.

To my surprise, I was instead handed an iPad to set up my patient profile. First reaction: great move to electronic records, doc!

The problem? To login, I was given a generic password (along the lines of “Password123!”). But I was never asked to change my password to something unique after logging in.

Let’s talk about why this is concerning. The purpose of authentication (i.e. entering a password) is to uniquely permit you to access your systems and information. So it should only be known…to you, right?

And here’s the thing: a new report from IDC predicts that “one in three U.S. healthcare customers will have their health records breached in 2016.” As more and more doctors move to online health records due to federal incentive funding (which has a myriad of benefits), our collective exposure increases. So this problem will only get worse.

But there are some steps you can take to keep your personal information safe and make you a less attractive target, even while healthcare organizations work to evolve their security practices.

  1. Check to make sure that the login page of your doctor’s web portal is securely authenticated, typically designated with “https:” and a green lock. For more about authentication, watch this 1-minute video from Symphony’s own Cryptoboy.

  2. Create a strong password. You’ve heard it before - your password should include at least 8 characters, and a mix of letters, numbers, uppercase/lowercase and special characters.

  3. More importantly, choose a password that’s hard to guess. ‘Password1234!’ might pass the checks above, but it would be awfully easy for a hacker to crack. Out of ideas? Try a rhyme!

  4. Finally - if you’re ever given a temporary password to access your account, change it right away to a unique, strong password known only to you.

Breaches are an unfortunate reality of life today. As more of our personal information moves online, don’t just rely on others to keep it safe, take control!
