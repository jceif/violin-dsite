Launching Symphony’s commitment to gender equality

Kim Tran

It’s one thing to have values — it’s another thing to act on them. One of Symphony’s core values is that “Diversity makes for a richer experience.” As a woman leader at this company, I will own making that value a reality by building a diverse workforce that reflects our evolving culture. It’s an ongoing challenge that we pose to ourselves not only due to the lack of gender and ethnic diversity in Silicon Valley,1 but because we know that a commitment to diversity enriches our product and will make Symphony more successful. According to entrepreneur and former Reddit CEO, Ellen Pao,2 “gender-diverse companies show 15 percent [greater] likelihood of financial outperformance.”

Today is Women’s Equality Day,3 and in honor of the day, we’re going for total transparency by sharing a frank analysis of our own hiring practices. What did the data tell us? We’re not perfect. In fact, in some ways, we are far from perfect. Here’s the current gender breakdown in terms of representation in executive and leadership roles, and across our total workforce:

We want to be honest with ourselves while we’re still fairly small. That way, as we grow from ~188 employees into a company of 1000, we’re doing so on a foundation that reflects and embraces diversity, and ensures that women and underrepresented minorities are paid equally.

Thankfully, I’m not the only executive investing in this idea. Our CEO, David Gurle, recognizes the need to go beyond pay to ensure we’re building a place where women can thrive. To that end, our benefits include six-month maternity leave, three-month paternity leave, and flexible work schedules for families. Family and work have an equal place in our organization and asking women (or men) to put their families on hold is just not in our DNA.

In the coming six months, our goal is to increase women’s representation across our team by three percent. Here are some of the initiatives we are launching to get there:

--Kick off an internal mentorship, sponsorship, and networking program

--Deliver focused training for managers on how to overcome unconscious bias4 during the hiring process (to complement existing recruitment communications)

--Increase company-wide awareness on our need for diversity and equality through communication, team events, and internal audits (like this one)

--Initiate the Kaizen5 model of continuous improvement: plan, do, check, act.

These goals go hand in hand with another Symphony core value: “We’ve never arrived.” We’re driven by the realization that we can always do better. The only way to reach our equality goals is to proceed with complete transparency and accountability — and it starts today!

Ok, enough gabbing — let’s meet some of the amazing women who are helping to build Symphony. :)

Shwetha: Every day when I step into the Symphony office, I know I'll have my wonderful team present to support and encourage me, all the while being silly about tiny things too. It is the little things that I experience here, like bad jokes, sudden revelry in mundane things, and just enjoying our time at work, that make me happy and glad to be part of such a strong team of men and women.
Aileen: I am fortunate to have the opportunity to lead a team that works on some of the most exciting and influential projects at Symphony. I definitely feel empowered as a female leader, and encouraged to develop my skills and individuality.
Sashi: From the day I stepped through the door at Symphony, I have been given the opportunity to grow as an effective leader, working mother and individual contributor. Symphony provides an open forum for sharing ideas, continuous strategy validation and has helped guide my career trajectory so that it seems limitless. I am so proud of the team culture and energy at Symphony – we truly are a family!
Queenie: My favorite part of being a woman at Symphony is having the opportunity to work closely with such a diverse team of intelligent, creative and driven women. We love our work as much as we love having fun and spending time with family, and this is always evident in the office atmosphere; We can seamlessly transition from discussing serious business proposals to planning social events outside the office!
Leah: At Symphony, I’m part of a team that is shaping future workplace dynamics and positively impacting women across global organizations. Symphony provides an empowering platform where all team members can connect and collaborate within their own personalized workspace, and I lend my female perspective to ensure that we design a product that suits the needs of female professionals just as much as our male counterparts.
Lily: As a woman working at Symphony, I’m not treated or spoken to differently than the men – everyone is respected equally. When I'm not demanding the best from my team during work, I school them on the basketball court every chance I get. The Symphony team is gender blind and this is a huge advantage when it comes to hiring the crème de la crème; Symphony's success will be a slam dunk!
Samantha: As a minority woman working in tech, I have been fortunate to be supported and mentored by both women and men throughout my career. I am grateful to those individuals for their advice, friendship and role in my journey so far; strong mentorship is invaluable. Today, I lead corporate communications at Symphony and feel respected and empowered by my colleagues across the organization.
Kim: Early in my career, I was lucky to be mentored by two powerhouse women. Out of the hundreds of lessons they taught me, probably their biggest influence on me was how they mentored. Even when I thought I knew it all they didn’t knock down my confidence. They guided me through patient questioning, a method I now use in my own mentoring. Watching my mentees grow has been a priceless gift.
